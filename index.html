Florida 2026 at a Glance

For the first time since 2018, Florida's top job is wide open: Gov. Ron DeSantis is term-limited. Voters will also fill a U.S. Senate seat vacated by Marco Rubio (now Secretary of State), the entire state Cabinet, the full U.S. House delegation, half the state Senate, all 120 state House seats, and at least two statewide constitutional amendments.

Closed primary state. Florida is one of 10 states with a fully closed presidential and party primary. To vote in the August 18 Republican or Democratic primary you must be registered with that party by July 20, 2026. No-party-affiliation (NPA) voters can only vote in nonpartisan races and ballot questions on the primary ballot. Everyone can vote in November.

The Big Themes of 2026

🏡 Property Taxes & Affordability

Gov. DeSantis spent the spring pushing to phase out property taxes on homesteaded property; the House passed HJR 203 (80–30) but it died in the Senate. Expect candidates to revive the fight — and the related fights over insurance premiums, rent, and housing supply.

🌊 Insurance Crisis

Florida has the highest homeowners-insurance premiums in the country, plus a fragile property-insurance market post-Hurricane Ian and Milton. Every serious candidate has a plan; none is small.

🛂 Immigration & "Alligator Alcatraz"

Florida is at the front of the second Trump administration's deportation push. The state is building a controversial detention facility in the Everglades and has expanded ICE cooperation. Candidates split sharply.

🩺 Abortion & Reproductive Care

Florida's 6-week abortion ban took full effect after voters rejected Amendment 4 in 2024 (57% Yes — short of the 60% threshold). The next governor inherits enforcement and any future ballot fight.

📚 Education & Schools

Universal school choice is now law; the live fight is teacher pay, voucher impact on traditional districts, curriculum (Stop WOKE successor cases), and book challenges.

🌴 Environment & Water

Everglades restoration, red tide and algal blooms, agricultural runoff, and a new front: opposition to massive AI data centers that strain Florida's water and grid.

Tip: Florida's Cabinet — Attorney General, Chief Financial Officer, and Commissioner of Agriculture — sits on key boards (clemency, FDLE, lands, finance). All three seats currently have appointed incumbents on the ballot for their first elected term.

Attorney General

Top state lawyer; defends state laws; oversees consumer protection, civil rights, and statewide prosecutions. James Uthmeier was appointed AG in 2025 after Moody moved to the Senate.

Chief Financial Officer (CFO)

Florida's chief financial officer; runs the state Department of Financial Services and oversees insurance regulation, accounting, and state investments. Blaise Ingoglia was appointed in 2025.

Commissioner of Agriculture & Consumer Services

Heads the Florida Department of Agriculture & Consumer Services — regulates farms, food safety, wildfire response, weights and measures, and concealed-carry permits. Wilton Simpson is the elected incumbent.

Statewide Constitutional Amendments

Heads up — these are on the November 3, 2026 General Election ballot. Florida's August 18 primary contains no statewide ballot measures. As of May 2026, two constitutional amendments are certified, both placed by the Legislature. Each requires 60% Yes votes to pass — a threshold Florida added in 2006.

Amendment 1 — Tangible Property Used for Agriculture & Agritourism

Placed by the Florida Legislature (HJR / SJR — legislatively referred). Final ballot title from the Secretary of State pending official certification.

What it does: Amends the Florida Constitution to exempt certain tangible personal property used in agriculture or agritourism — farm equipment, tools, machinery — from ad valorem (property) taxes, when the property is: habitually located or typically present on agricultural land; used for producing agricultural products or for agritourism; and owned by the landowner or leaseholder.

Why it's on the ballot: Florida lawmakers say current rules tax farm equipment as commercial business property, which they argue penalizes family farms competing with out-of-state imports. The Legislature passed it with broad bipartisan support — roughly 99% of Republicans and 87% of Democrats in floor votes.

YES means: Farm and agritourism equipment is exempted from property taxes statewide — saving farmers money but reducing tax revenue to counties, school districts, and special taxing districts.
NO means: Current rules stay in place. Counties continue assessing tangible-property taxes on farm and agritourism equipment, with no statewide exemption.
Threshold: 60% Yes required to pass. Effective date if passed: January 1, 2027 (typical for FL legislatively referred property-tax amendments).

Amendment 2 — Changes to the Budget Stabilization Fund

Placed by the Florida Legislature (referred June 16, 2025). Ballot title pending.

What it does: Increases the maximum amount Florida can keep in its Budget Stabilization Fund (the "rainy day fund") from 10% of net general revenue to 25%. Requires the Legislature to transfer the lesser of $750 million or 25% of general revenue collections into the fund each year. Allows the Legislature to suspend the transfer in three cases: covering revenue shortfalls; emergency spending; or a two-thirds vote determining a "critical need" supersedes the transfer (when the fund balance is >15% of revenue collections).

Supporters say: Florida Senate President Ben Albritton: the amendment "makes Florida's balance sheet more durable in difficult times." House Speaker Daniel Perez: "preparation for the unexpected" — citing the 2000s recession.

Opponents say: Rich Templin (FL AFL-CIO): "Putting money into an account that may or may never be used as opposed to funding kids having access to healthcare, or education, or teacher salaries."

YES means: Florida sets aside up to 25% of general revenue in reserves and locks in an annual deposit — larger rainy-day cushion, less money available for current programs.
NO means: Current 10% cap stays. Florida keeps a smaller reserve and retains more flexibility on current-year spending.
Threshold: 60% Yes required to pass. Vote totals in Legislature: passed with broad bipartisan support.
Watch for late additions. If the Legislature passes a property-tax-elimination amendment in a summer 2026 special session (with 60% supermajorities in both chambers), it could still be added to the November ballot before the Secretary of State's late-August certification deadline. Several citizen initiatives are also gathering signatures — including a Voting Rights Initiative and an Electronic-Signatures Initiative — but none had qualified for the ballot as of late spring 2026.

Local Elections — By County

Beyond statewide races, your county ballot includes county commission, school board, county court judge, and (in some places) sheriff, mayor, or charter amendment races. Select your county below for a snapshot.

Florida's local cycle is staggered. Sheriff, State Attorney, Public Defender, Tax Collector, Property Appraiser, Clerk of Court, and Supervisor of Elections were on the ballot in 2024 for most counties — their next election is 2028. The big 2026 county races are County Commission and School Board, plus county court judges. Notable exceptions are flagged below.
Redistricting alert. Gov. DeSantis signed a new congressional map on May 4, 2026, reworking 21 of Florida's 28 U.S. House districts. The 2026 elections are the first under that map. State legislative districts were redrawn in 2022 and remain in effect. Twenty of 40 state Senate seats (even-numbered) and all 120 state House seats are on the 2026 ballot. How to Vote in Florida

Florida runs partisan closed primaries, then a general election. You don't need a reason to vote by mail, and early voting is mandatory in every county.

📅 Key Dates — Primary

July 20: Last day to register or switch party affiliation.

Aug 6: Last day to request a vote-by-mail ballot.

Aug 8–15: Mandatory early-voting period.

Aug 18: Primary Election Day — polls 7 a.m. – 7 p.m.

📅 Key Dates — General

Oct 5: Last day to register to vote.

Oct 22: Last day to request a vote-by-mail ballot.

Oct 24–31: Mandatory early-voting period.

Nov 3: General Election Day — polls 7 a.m. – 7 p.m.

✅ Am I Registered?

Check status at registration.elections.myflorida.com

Register or update party at registertovoteflorida.gov

Florida has no same-day registration — meet the deadline.

📬 Vote by Mail

Any registered voter can request a mail ballot — no excuse needed.

Request through your county Supervisor of Elections.

Returned ballots must be received by 7 p.m. on Election Day — postmark does NOT count.

🗳 Vote in Person

Find your early-voting and Election Day polling place: dos.fl.gov

Bring a current valid photo + signature ID (driver's license, FL ID, U.S. passport, military ID, etc.).

⚖️ Closed-Primary Rule

To vote in the Republican or Democratic primary on August 18, you must be registered with that party by July 20.

NPA voters (no party affiliation) only vote in nonpartisan races on the primary ballot.

All registered voters can vote in November.

📖 Official Resources

Florida Division of Elections: dos.fl.gov/elections

County Supervisors of Elections (your local office runs the election): find yours here

🛂 Bring ID

Florida requires photo + signature ID at the polls. Accepted forms include FL driver license, FL ID, U.S. passport, military ID, student ID, employee ID, concealed-carry license, retirement-community ID, neighborhood-association ID, public-assistance ID, veteran-health-ID, license to carry, Government employee ID.
